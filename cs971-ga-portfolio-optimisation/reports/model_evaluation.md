## Risk-Return Preference Sweep

The preference sweep uses `lambda * return - (1 - lambda) * risk`, with `lambda` in `[0, 1]`. Low `lambda` values emphasise risk reduction, while high values emphasise return. The top-weight table generated by the notebook should be interpreted as an asset-rotation diagnostic: persistent names across multiple `lambda` values are more robust, while names that appear only under high-return settings are more sensitive to the chosen preference.

## Asset Selection Experiment

The asset-selection stage compares a proxy selection approach with an integrated nested GA approach.

| Method | Training Return | Training Risk | Training Sharpe | Holdout Return | Holdout Risk | Holdout Sharpe |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Proxy selection + equal weights | 0.2436 | 0.1529 | 1.5936 | 0.3093 | 0.1816 | 1.7031 |
| Proxy selection + GA weights | 0.2883 | 0.1528 | 1.8862 | 0.3162 | 0.1872 | 1.6885 |
| Integrated selection + GA weights | 0.2871 | 0.1744 | 1.6457 | 0.2108 | 0.1904 | 1.1073 |

The integrated method is computationally more expensive because each selected subset receives weight-optimisation signal. Its value is methodological: asset selection and weight optimisation are linked directly instead of being treated as isolated stages.

## Evaluation Notes

The strongest evidence for generalisation comes from holdout-period comparisons and random subset benchmarking, not from training-period Sharpe alone. The results suggest that evolutionary search can identify competitive allocations, but the method should be evaluated under additional market windows before being treated as robust.

## Limitations

- No transaction costs, taxes, slippage, liquidity limits, or turnover limits.
- Long-only fully invested portfolios only.
- Sharpe ratio and volatility may not capture all relevant downside risks.
- Results are sensitive to the chosen window, market regime, asset universe, and stochastic optimiser settings.
